"Virtual Pipelines" Transport Natural Gas Across the Country

By: Natalie Jonas

More than 3 million miles of natural gas pipelines move natural gas throughout the U.S. Yet, significant portions of the country — primarily rural, economically disadvantaged towns — lack such infrastructure, and therefore don't have reliable or cost-effective access to such energy. Virtual pipelines help address this gap. But, virtual pipelines are not subject to the same stringent oversight as traditional oil and gas systems, a regulatory gap that is often exploited.

Virtual pipelines use piecemeal infrastructure — trains, boats and trucks — as parts of a whole, allowing oil and gas to move and flow where it wasn't able to reach before. This method is cheaper than constructing pipelines, which is a lengthy and costly process, particularly in remote locations.

According to an analysis of the U.S. Energy Information Administration data, approximately 43% of natural gas was used to generate electric power, 29% for industrial use and an additional 16% for residential purposes, particularly heating.

U.S. Secretary of Energy Chris Wright announced the launch of the Trump Peace Pipelines Framework on April 28, noting a new infusion of billions of dollars into natural gas infrastructure in Central and Eastern Europe. The effort will allow such areas the capacity to import higher amounts of liquefied natural gas (LNG) from the U.S. There are nearly 400 natural gas storage sites in the U.S., which largely hold LNG extracted from the Gulf Coast, states such as Texas and Louisiana. Eight terminals in the U.S. ship LNG to the rest of the world — Europe imports 69% of the U.S.'s LNG, up 35% from the year prior to Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Five new terminals will open in the U.S. by the end of 2027.

The closure and turmoil surrounding LNG movement through the Strait of Hormuz has sent prices soaring, the most disruptive event in the natural gas market since Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Natural gas supplies nearly 23% of global energy and is becoming even more valuable due to its ability to take different forms and move around easier, in some cases, than oil or other energy sources — abetted by a burgeoning network of virtual pipelines.

Extracted natural gas can be frozen, compressed, liquified and reheated, travelling an average of 506 miles, upwards of 1,864 miles, from its origin to locations within the U.S. But, the advent of resource-intensive data centers in remote locations along with the ongoing market squeeze is spurring on increased investment in virtual pipelines, which is expected to grow from $172 billion in 2025 to $1.92 billion by 2034, according to Fortune Business Insights.

Compressed natural gas (CNG) and LNG are most commonly transported using virtual pipelines. Compressed and liquefied natural gas is kept in a "boiling cryogen" state in fortified containers kept below -259.6°F.

These tanks are transported on boats, trains and trucks — or a combination of the three.

Natural gas is combustible and susceptible to explosion if it mixes with oxygen, is exposed to heat or escapes containment. The approval of natural gas transportation via railroads in 2020, under the first Trump administration, raised fears of "bomb trains." The frigid temperature LNG and CNG are kept at also pose potential risks.

The Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration (PHMSA) oversees the safety and regulation of virtual pipelines. Traditional pipelines in the U.S. are subject to the stringent regulation under the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission, but virtual pipelines are not. Vermont, which has the least gas pipeline infrastructure of any state in the continental U.S., relies on virtual pipelines. Nearly 68% of LNG and CNG is transported on trucks in virtual pipelines.

Multiple reports of accidents involving virtual pipelines were reported in 2024. Two trucks owned by NG Advantage, laden with containers of compressed natural gas, broke out in flames that year. NG Advantage, the first company to market a "virtual pipeline" in the U.S., is just one player in the growing field. NG Advantage operates multiple routes, one of which involves the daily transportation of compressed methane on trucks that leave from Milton, Vermont, headed to a paper mill nearly 70 miles away in Ticonderoga, New York.

Proposed pipeline systems, such as the Keystone XL and Dakota Access, have been successfully challenged by activists. The proliferation of virtual pipeline systems creates new methods to move natural gas in the absence of pipeline mega-projects. But, the piecemeal movement of natural gas is not subject to the regulatory oversight physical pipelines are, despite virtual pipelines potentially presenting even more harmful environmental risks.
